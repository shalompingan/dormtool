7 Roommate Rules That'll Save Your Friendship and Your Security Deposit

✍️ By ChrisJune 29, 2026 · 9 min readRoommate Guide

You met your future roommate on a Facebook group. You clicked instantly — same major, same music taste, same hatred for 8 AM classes. You're convinced this will be the best living situation ever.

Then, week three: they haven't done dishes in five days. Their partner has basically moved in. And somehow the utility bill is "late" because nobody knows whose turn it was to pay.

According to a 2025 survey by the National Apartment Association, 47% of roommate conflicts are about money (bills, rent split, shared purchases), and over 30% of college roommate relationships end with at least one person feeling resentful. The common thread? Nobody set ground rules upfront.

Here are 7 specific rules to establish with your roommate before move-in day. They'll save your friendship, your bank account, and your security deposit.

📝 Rule 1: Put It in Writing — The Roommate Agreement

Verbal agreements are worth the pixels they're spoken into. Write it down.

Why a Written Agreement Matters

When you agree on something verbally, everyone remembers it slightly differently. A month later, you'll hear "I never said I'd pay by the 5th." A written roommate agreement eliminates the memory problem. It doesn't need to be a legal contract — a Google Doc or shared note that you both sign is enough. But it needs to exist before you move in together.
What to include: Rent amount and who pays whom, utility split (electric, internet, water), which bills are in whose name, chore schedule, quiet hours, guest policy, food sharing rules, and how you'll handle disputes (simple majority wins? mediator?). Be specific about deadlines — "rent is due by the 5th, venmo by 11:59 PM."

💰 Rule 2: Split Bills Like an Adult

Money is the #1 cause of roommate conflict. Make it boring and automatic.

How to Split Rent and Utilities

Rent: Split evenly by the number of roommates unless bedrooms are significantly different sizes. If one room is much bigger or has a private bathroom, agree on a proportional split (e.g., 55/45 for two people). Document this before lease signing — renegotiating after you move in is awkward.
Utilities: Split evenly. Yes, even if you're gone for winter break. Utility bills don't go to zero. Exception: if someone works from home and the AC runs 8 hours more per day, discuss an adjusted split. Best practice: put each utility in one person's name and have everyone reimburse them monthly. Rotate who holds which bill so no one carries all the financial risk.
Shared supplies: Toilet paper, paper towels, dish soap, trash bags, cleaning supplies. Decide: do you buy a shared pool (split costs evenly) or buy your own and label them? The shared pool option is simpler but requires everyone to chip in without being asked.

🔇 Rule 3: Set Quiet Hours & Noise Expectations

You and your roommate probably have different definitions of "late." Agree on hours before someone's sleep suffers.

Define Quiet Hours in Writing

Standard approach: Quiet hours from 10 PM to 8 AM on weeknights (Sunday–Thursday) and midnight to 9 AM on weekends (Friday–Saturday). During quiet hours: no loud music or TV, no vacuuming, no groups in common areas, phone calls in bedrooms only. Headphones are required for anything audio after 10 PM.
Be specific about what "quiet" means. Can someone have a friend over talking at normal volume? Can someone cook? The more specific you are, the fewer disagreements. "No unreasonable noise" is too vague — your 11 PM study group with light conversation might be their insomnia trigger.

What About Different Schedules?

If one roommate works nights or has early morning classes while the other stays up late, negotiate a compromise. Morning person: be quiet getting ready (lay out clothes the night before, use a silent alarm). Night person: use headphones, cook earlier, prep everything before quiet hours start. The key is mutual respect — both sides need to compromise a little.

🧹 Rule 4: Create a Chore Schedule (And Stick to It)

The "I'll clean when it bothers me" approach works for exactly one person. Agree on who does what and when.

The Minimum Viable Chore System

Weekly chores (rotate every week): vacuum/sweep common areas, clean kitchen counters and sink, take out trash and recycling, wipe bathroom surfaces and toilet, sweep bathroom floor. Monthly chores (same person each month or rotate): deep clean fridge, clean oven/microwave inside, dust blinds and baseboards, wash windows. Daily habits: wash your own dishes immediately, wipe sink after use, remove hair from shower drain after you shower.
Three systems that work: 1) Rotating schedule — switch chores weekly so nobody gets stuck with the worst job permanently. 2) Divide and conquer — one person always does kitchen, the other always does bathroom (good if you have different cleanliness standards for different areas). 3) Hire a cleaner — split the cost ($40–80/person/month) and never fight about cleaning again. Option 3 is surprisingly popular with graduate students and working professionals.

How to Handle the "I'll Do It Later" Roommate

Agree on a maximum response time: 24 hours for dishes in the sink, 48 hours for taking out trash, 72 hours for vacuuming. If the deadline passes, the chore falls to the next person on the rotation — but the original person covers that person's chore next time. This creates accountability without passive-aggressive sticky notes.

👥 Rule 5: Guest & Overnight Visitor Policy

The roommate who never has guests over will eventually live with someone whose partner never leaves. Find the middle ground.

What a Fair Guest Policy Looks Like

Daytime guests: any time, no permission needed, but give a heads-up via text. No guests during quiet hours after 10 PM without agreement. Overnight guests: max 2–3 consecutive nights per week. Notify roommate at least 24 hours in advance. Same guest can't stay more than 3 nights in a row without everyone agreeing. House rules for guests: guests follow your apartment rules (quiet hours, shoes off, etc.). The host is responsible for their guest's behavior. If a guest is rude or disrespectful, the host handles it.

The Partner Conversation

When someone gets into a serious relationship, overnight frequency often increases. This is a normal tension point. Schedule a honest check-in: "Hey, Partner is spending 5 nights a week now. I'm happy for you, but our utility costs have gone up and I feel like I have less privacy. Can we talk about adjusting the arrangement?" Possible solutions: partner contributes to utilities if staying 3+ nights/week, partner has a key for convenience (if everyone agrees), or set a weekly overnight limit that works for both of you.
💡 Pro tip: If one roommate's partner basically lives there, it's fair to adjust utility and even rent split. A third person using water, electricity, and common space isn't free. Have this conversation early — it's much harder to bring up after 3 months of resentment.

🍕 Rule 6: Set Food & Shared Supply Boundaries

Few things cause more daily irritation than someone eating your leftovers. Establish food rules week one.

Three Food Systems That Work

System 1 — Label everything: Buy your own food, label it with your name, and never touch what isn't yours. Each person has their own shelf in the fridge and cabinet. Simple, clear, but zero flexibility. System 2 — Shared basics, personal everything else: Everyone contributes equally to a shared fund for basics (milk, eggs, bread, cooking oil, coffee, toilet paper). Everything else is personal and off-limits. This is the most common system because it balances convenience with boundaries. System 3 — Full share: Everything is shared, you split grocery costs equally. This works for people with similar diets and eating habits, but falls apart fast if someone eats twice as much or has dietary restrictions that cost more.

The One Rule That Prevents 80% of Food Conflicts

If you finish it, replace it. That milk you used for your cereal? If there's a quarter left, buy a new one within 24 hours. That butter you used for toast? Replace it. This single rule — finish it, replace it — eliminates the majority of food-related roommate resentment. 🔑 Rule 7: Plan Your Security Deposit Exit From Day One

The average dorm move-out fine is $85–200. Off-campus apartment deductions average $200–400. Don't lose your deposit to preventable damage.

Document Everything Before Move-In

On move-in day, walk through the entire apartment with your phone. Take video of every room and close-up photos of every existing scratch, dent, stain, and crack. Time-stamped photos are your best defense against false damage claims. Upload everything to a shared folder (Google Drive or Dropbox) that both roommates and the landlord can access. Have the landlord sign or email-confirm the condition report.
What to photograph specifically: walls (scratches, holes, marks), floors (stains, scratches, carpet burns), windows (cracks, broken seals), countertops (burns, cuts), appliances (dents, missing parts), toilet/shower (cracks, mold), blinds/curtains (broken slats, missing pieces). Get the same lighting conditions you'll have at move-out so your photos are directly comparable.

Agree on Move-Out Responsibilities Now

Decide upfront: will everyone clean their own room and split common areas, or will you hire professional cleaners and split the cost? Professional cleaning is $150–$300 for a standard apartment — split three ways, that's $50–100 per person and removes all arguments about "your side of the bathroom is dirtier."
Before move-out, schedule a walk-through with all roommates present. Check the damage list together, fill any nail holes, touch up paint, and clean everything. If there's damage, agree on how to handle it before the landlord does the inspection. Roommates who blame each other in front of the landlord often end up with everyone getting deducted.

Security Deposit Split Agreement

Agree in writing: if the full deposit is returned, it's split evenly (or proportionally if rent was split unevenly). If there are deductions, they come from the deposit before splitting. Exception: if damage is clearly one person's fault (that red wine stain on the carpet in their bedroom), that person covers the deduction from their share. Use a shared note or the Bill Splitter to document who gets how much when the deposit check comes.

✦ ✦ ✦

The bottom line on roommate rules: The best time to set ground rules is before you move in. The second-best time is today. Write it down, split bills fairly with a tracking tool, respect each other's sleep and space, clean up after yourself, and document your apartment condition on day one. Follow these 7 rules and you'll come out of the lease with your friendship — and your full security deposit — intact.

📌 Quick Recap — Roommate Rules

  • 📝 Written agreement: Cover rent, bills, chores, guests, and quiet hours before move-in day
  • 💰 Split bills fairly: Use a tracking tool so everyone sees who paid and who owes
  • 🔇 Respect quiet hours: Weeknights 10 PM–8 AM, weekends midnight–9 AM (adjust as needed)
  • 🧹 Chore schedule: Rotate weekly or divide permanently — agree on a system
  • 👥 Guest policy: Max 2–3 overnight nights/week, 24-hour notice, host is responsible
  • 🍕 Food boundaries: If you finish it, replace it. Label or split shared supplies
  • 🔑 Document everything: Photos + condition report on move-in day = full deposit back
