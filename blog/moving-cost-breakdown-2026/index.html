How Much Does It Actually Cost to Move Into a Dorm or Apartment? (2026 Breakdown)

✍️ By ChrisJune 29, 2026 · 8 min readMoving Costs

You got the acceptance letter. You signed the lease. Now a new question is creeping in: how much is this actually going to cost?

Between the housing deposit, furniture, supplies, and the random stuff you didn't know you needed — the upfront cost of moving out for the first time hits a lot of students harder than expected. The problem is, most cost breakdowns online are written by people who haven't been a student in 15 years and quote prices from 2019.

Based on current 2026 pricing data, moving into a dorm costs $1,200–$2,500 in one-time expenses. Moving into a first apartment costs $3,000–$6,000 upfront. In both cases, about 40% of that spending is on things first-timers don't think about until move-in day.

This guide breaks down every dollar — dorm vs. apartment, category by category, with real 2026 prices. Use it to budget before you start shopping, and you'll avoid the #1 mistake: running out of money before you have everything you need.

🏠 Dorm Move-In Costs (One-Time)

These are the upfront costs when moving into a college dorm, excluding tuition and housing fees which are billed separately.

Dorm Essentials & Supplies: $300–$800

This includes your bedding (mattress topper, sheets, pillows, comforter — about $100–250), bathroom supplies (shower caddy, towels, shower shoes, toiletries — $50–100), kitchen items (mini fridge and microwave if your dorm allows them and you coordinate with your roommate — $100–300), storage (bins, Command hooks, hangers — $40–80), cleaning supplies ($20–40), and random essentials like a power strip, fan, and desk lamp ($40–80). The biggest variable is whether you buy a mini fridge and microwave — coordinate with your roommate to avoid two of each.

Technology: $200–$600

You likely already have a laptop, but you'll need accessories: noise-canceling headphones or earbuds ($25–$80 for budget picks, $150–$350 for premium), a laptop stand ($15–25), an ethernet cable ($8–15 — dorm Wi-Fi is notoriously unreliable), a portable power bank ($20–30), and a printer if your school requires one ($40–80). Skip the printer if your campus offers free printing — most do.

Moving Logistics: $100–$400

Gas money if you're driving to campus ($30–150 depending on distance), moving supplies (boxes, tape, markers — $20–50), food for move-in day (snacks, water, pizza for helpers — $20–50), and luggage shipping if you're flying ($50–150 with LugLess or similar services).

First Week Expenses: $80–$150

This is the category everyone forgets. You'll need to buy groceries for your dorm room snacks ($30–50), cover unexpected orientation costs ($10–30), pay for laundry ($20–40 in quarters — most dorm machines take quarters or cards), and buy school supplies you didn't know you needed ($20–50). Bring $60–100 in cash — the laundry card machine is always broken and only takes cash.

🏢 First Apartment Move-In Costs

Moving into an apartment costs significantly more upfront — here's where every dollar goes.

Security Deposit + First Month's Rent: $1,500–$3,000

This is the biggest upfront cost. Most apartments require first month's rent plus a security deposit (usually equal to one month's rent). In 2026, the average studio/one-bedroom rent in the US is about $1,200–$1,800 depending on location. Some apartments also require last month's rent upfront — ask before applying. Pro tip: Use our Rent Affordability Calculator before signing anything to make sure you can actually afford the monthly payment.

Furniture: $1,000–$3,000

Starting from zero is expensive. The biggest items: mattress + bed frame ($200–600), sofa ($200–800 new, $50–200 secondhand), kitchen table and chairs ($100–300), dresser ($80–250), and a desk ($50–200). Shop Facebook Marketplace for at least 50–70% of your furniture. Coffee tables, nightstands, dressers, and even sofas are widely available for a fraction of retail. Buy new: mattress, pillows, sheets, and towels. Everything else can be secondhand.

Kitchen & Household Supplies: $400–$800

This is the category that surprises most new renters. You need: cookware set ($40–80), knives + cutting board ($25–40), plates/bowls/mugs/silverware ($50–80), basic utensils ($15–25), microwave ($50–80), food storage ($15–25), dish rack + soap + sponges ($20–30), trash can + bags ($25–40), cleaning supplies (vacuum, broom, cleaners — $60–120), and a shower curtain + liner + hooks ($15–30). If you use our First Apartment Checklist, you can track all of these and see the estimated budget.

Utility Setup & Connection Fees: $100–$300

Setting up utilities for the first time requires deposits: electric ($50–150), gas ($30–100 if applicable), water/sewer ($30–100 if billed separately), and internet ($50–100 installation fee). Some providers waive the deposit if you have good credit. Ask about "budget billing" — it averages your payments so you don't get a $400 electric bill in August from running the AC.

Renter's Insurance: $10–$20/month

Many landlords require it. Even if they don't, you should get it. Renter's insurance covers your belongings against fire, theft, water damage, and liability. It costs about $120–200 per year and is the cheapest peace of mind you can buy. Companies like Lemonade, State Farm, and Progressive offer quick online quotes.

💣 10 Hidden Costs Nobody Mentions

These are the expenses that don't show up on any checklist — but they will show up on your credit card.

1. First Grocery Stock-Up: $100–$200

Filling a fridge and pantry from empty is shockingly expensive. Spices, oil, condiments, coffee, rice, pasta — the basics alone cost $50. Then add fresh food, frozen food, snacks, and drinks. Budget $150 for your first grocery trip and expect to be surprised.

2. Cleaning Supplies (Full Kit): $50–$100

Vacuum, broom, mop, all-purpose cleaner, disinfecting wipes, microfiber cloths, toilet cleaner, glass cleaner, sponges, trash bags, laundry detergent, dryer sheets — these all come individually and add up fast.

3. Tools & Hardware: $30–$60

Tool kit ($15–25), Command hooks ($8–12), scissors ($5), flashlight + batteries ($10–15), fire extinguisher ($15–25), and a tape measure ($5). You won't think about these until you need them.

4. Curtains & Window Coverings: $30–$100

Most apartments come with bare windows or cheap blinds. Curtains + rods for 2–3 windows cost $30–100 depending on quality. Measure your windows before buying — standard sizes don't always fit.

5. Light Bulbs: $10–$20

Your apartment may have bulbs, but they'll be the harshest possible temperature. Replacing with warm LED bulbs costs $10–20 and makes a huge difference.

6. First Aid & Medicine Cabinet: $20–$40

Band-aids, ibuprofen, allergy meds, cold medicine, antacids, thermometer. You won't need them until 9 PM on a Sunday when CVS is closed.

7. Trash Cans (Every Room): $40–$80

You need at least 3: kitchen (13-gallon, $15–25), bathroom (small with lid, $8–15), and desk/bedroom (small, $8–15). Plus trash bags for each size.

8. Shower Curtain + Liner + Rings + Bath Mat: $25–$50

Your apartment has a shower rod. That's it. Everything else is on you. Get a fabric curtain, a plastic liner (PEVA), rings/hooks, and a non-slip bath mat.

9. Change of Address + Mail Forwarding: $5–$15

USPS mail forwarding is technically free, but you'll spend money on new address labels, updating subscriptions, and replacing your driver's license or registration if it's tied to your old address.

10. The "I Forgot Something" Tax: $50–$100

Every single person who moves out for the first time makes at least 2–3 additional trips to Target/Walmart in the first week. Things you forgot: a can opener, scissors, a plunger (please don't forget the plunger), a dish drying rack, trash bags, and a shower caddy. Budget a $50–100 buffer for the things you'll inevitably realize you need.

💰 How to Save 30%+ on Move-In Costs

Most first-time movers overspend by hundreds. Here's where to cut without cutting quality.

1. Buy Furniture Secondhand (Save $500–$1,000)

This is the single biggest money-saver. New sofa: $500–800. Used sofa on Facebook Marketplace: $50–200. Same for coffee tables, nightstands, dressers, desks, and lamps. The only things you should always buy new: mattress, pillows, sheets, towels, and kitchen sponges. Everything else can be secondhand. Check for bed bugs before bringing any secondhand furniture inside — look for dark spots in seams and small blood stains.

2. Use Our Checklists to Avoid Duplicates (Save $100–$300)

The #1 mistake first-time movers make is buying duplicates because they forgot what they already bought. Use our First Apartment Checklist or Dorm Checklist to track every purchase in real time. Check items off as you buy them, and share the link with family so they don't buy you a third set of sheets.

3. Coordinate With Roommates (Save $100–$400)

One mini fridge per apartment, not two. One microwave. One vacuum. One set of cleaning supplies. Use our Roommate Bill Splitter to split shared purchases evenly — and avoid the awkward "you owe me $40 for the mop" conversation.

4. Wait on Non-Essentials (Save $200–$500)

You don't need everything on day one. Week 1 priority: mattress, sheets, pillows, towel, toiletries, kitchen basics (one pan, one pot, one plate, one bowl, one mug, one set of utensils), trash can, cleaning supplies. Week 2–4: sofa (you'll survive sitting on the floor for a week), coffee table, TV, decor, rug, extra kitchen gadgets. Month 2+: decorative items, plant, extra furniture, wall art. Spreading purchases over 2 months makes the cost manageable.
