What Not to Bring to College: 5 Dorm Packing Mistakes Freshmen Make

✍️ By ChrisJune 27, 2026 · 7 min readPacking Guide

You've seen the dorm tour videos. The twinkle lights draped like a fairy kingdom. The matching beige comforter set. The "dorm aesthetic" that looks like a West Elm catalog threw up in a 12x12 cinderblock box. Cute content. Real talk? Half that stuff will be shoved under your bed by week two, and you'll be out $300 on things that actively make your life worse.

I've been through it. I've watched freshmen show up with trunk organizers and leave with them still empty at winter break. I've seen the $80 "dorm rug" get destroyed by dining hall floor grease within a month. I've done the walk of shame returning a full garbage bag of useless junk to Target.

This is your what not to bring to college dorm first year checklist — from someone who learned the hard way so you don't have to. Skip these five mistakes and your dorm packing list will be half the size and twice as useful.

1. A Giant Desk Printer — The #1 Dorm Packing Mistake

You will print approximately 6 pages all semester. We promise.

Leave at Home

A Desk Printer (And the 6 Cartridges Your Mom Bought)

You think you'll print 50 pages a week. You won't. You will print six pages all semester — and four of them will be on the last day before break when you suddenly remember you need to submit a signed form. The ink cartridge will dry up by October. The printer will take up 30% of your desk, become a shelf for random papers, and when you finally need it, the magenta ink will be out even though you're printing in black and white. Every dorm building has a free or cheap printing station in the lobby. The library gives you a printing quota each semester. Your department office will print things for you if you ask. You are paying for access to these printers with your tuition — use them.

Buy Instead

Nothing. You already have printing access.

Take the $80 you would have spent on a printer and put it toward a solid mattress topper. Your back will remember the trade every single night.

2. A Full Set of Dishes and Cookware — What to Leave Home for College

Your meal plan is already paid for. Use it.

Leave at Home

12-Piece Dinnerware Set, Pots, Pans, and the Garlic Press You'll Never Use

I get it — your mom bought you a cute 12-piece dinnerware set from Target. It's a nice gesture. But here's the reality of your college packing list: you're eating in the dining hall for 90% of your meals. That meal swipe costs you (or your loans) $10–$15 whether you use it or not. You are not going to cook pasta at 11 PM in a communal kitchen that smells like burnt popcorn and last week's fish. You will use exactly one bowl, one mug, one spoon, and maybe a set of chopsticks for takeout container noodles.

Buy Instead

One large microwave-safe bowl, one spill-proof mug, one set of utensils.

That's it. If your dorm allows a mini-fridge, great. If not, you genuinely don't need one. The dining hall exists for a reason — it's included in your tuition for a reason. Let it do its job and save your limited desk space.

3. Your Full High School Wardrobe — Overpacking Is a Right of Passage

40 hangers worth of clothes for a closet that's 18 inches wide.

Leave at Home

The Letterman Jacket, Prom Dress, and 3 Hoodies From Clubs You Quit

You'll bring 40 items. You'll wear the same 8–10 outfits on eternal rotation, because that's literally what everyone does in college. Nobody is judging your outfit — they're all stressed about their own classes. The formal dress from prom, the letterman jacket from high school, the three hoodies from clubs you quit junior year — they will sit untouched in a laundry bag until move-out day. For your what not to bring to college dorm first year checklist, this is the hardest category because clothes feel essential. But they take up the most space in your car and the least-used space in your room.

Buy Instead

7 days of casual wear, 2 nicer outfits, 1 rain jacket, and shower flip-flops (non-negotiable).

Communal shower floors are chemically hazardous. Do not skip the flip-flops. Everything else — you can survive with less than you think.

4. Textbooks Before Syllabus Week — A $300 Mistake

Half your professors will change the "required" book by the first class.

Leave at Home

That $180 "Introduction to Psychology" Textbook You Bought in July

I was that overachiever. I bought "Introduction to Psychology" for $180 before the first class, only for the professor to walk in and say "actually we use open-source readings on Canvas this year." Half your professors will change the textbook between when you order it and when class starts. The other half will list a "required" textbook that's actually optional. You will spend $300 on books you crack open zero times. This might be the biggest money waster on your first year dorm checklist — it's certainly the most unnecessary.

Buy Instead

Nothing. Wait until Week 2. Then rent used or find a PDF.

Go to the first week of classes. Sit down. Take notes. Listen. On Friday, check the syllabus. If the book is truly required, rent it used from Amazon or your campus bookstore. Do not buy new. Do not buy before you know you need it. This one habit will save you more money than any packing decision you make all year.

5. A Car on Campus — The Hidden Cost of Convenience

Parking pass + insurance + anxiety = not worth it for freshmen.

Leave at Home

Your Car (Seriously, Leave It)

Freshman year, your entire life is within a half-mile radius. Dining hall? 3-minute walk. Classes? 5-minute walk. Library? 7-minute walk. The library, the quad, your friend's dorm — everything is walkable. A car means a parking pass ($300–$800 per year), an insurance bump, and the constant low-grade anxiety of "did someone hit my bumper in the student lot?" You don't need a car. You need a good backpack and maybe a bike. The car will sit in the lot collecting bird droppings while you Venmo your parents every semester for the parking renewal. This is the most expensive entry on your dorm packing list that isn't even a "packing" item — but it deserves a spot because so many freshmen make this mistake.

Buy Instead

A good backpack with laptop compartment, a 32oz water bottle, and a portable charger.

Put the car money toward a weekend trip with friends instead. You'll get more memories for less money.

✦ ✦ ✦

I wish someone had shown me a what not to bring to college dorm first year checklist before I packed my entire room into a trunk and regretted half of it. The problem is every "dorm guide" out there is written by a retailer trying to sell you stuff. Target wants you to buy the $60 storage cube system. Bed Bath & Beyond wants you to fill a cart. TikTok influencers want you to buy the aesthetic trash can they get a commission on.

Nobody is on your side telling you what to skip.

📌 Key Takeaways — Your Dorm Packing List Shortcut

  • Don't bring a printer — campus printers are free and everywhere
  • Don't bring full cookware — your meal plan covers almost everything
  • Don't bring your whole closet — 8 outfits on rotation is the norm
  • Don't buy textbooks before Week 2 — syllabus week changes everything
  • Don't bring a car freshman year — walking is faster and cheaper
