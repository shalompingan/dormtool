Your RA Wants to Fine You: 5 Dorm Move-Out Traps to Avoid in 2026

✍️ By ChrisJune 28, 2026 · 6 min readMove-Out Guide

You survived finals. You packed your bags. You think you're done.

Then the RA does a final room inspection, and a week later you get an email: "You have been charged $185 for damages and excessive cleaning."

This happens to thousands of college freshmen every May. The average dorm move-out fine in the US is $85–200 per student, and most of it comes from five specific traps that are incredibly easy to fix if you know about them beforehand.

Here's exactly what the RA is checking — and how to avoid dorm move out fees before they hit your student account.

📑 The 5 Traps
  1. Command Strip Damage on Walls
  2. Mystery Carpet Stains
  3. The Refrigerator That Grew a Science Experiment
  4. Broken or Stuck Blinds
  5. Trash and Personal Items Left Behind

Trap #1: Command Strip Damage on Walls

Typical fine: $25–50 per spot

What the RA does

They run their hand along every wall, especially above your bed and desk. If they feel torn paint, ripped drywall paper, or sticky residue — that's a charge per spot. Command strips are supposed to be "damage-free," but they're not if you rip them off wrong.

How to fix it

Pull the strip straight down along the wall, not outward. The adhesive releases when you stretch it horizontally, not when you yank it toward yourself.

If the strip tore off paint underneath: use a Magic Eraser to smooth the edge, then a small tub of spackling paste ($4 at Target) to fill the divot. Dab it on with your finger, wait 20 minutes, and sand gently with a fine-grit sponge.

For sticky residue left behind: rubbing alcohol or Goo Gone on a paper towel dissolves it in seconds without damaging the paint.

Trap #2: Mystery Carpet Stains

Typical fine: $50–150

What the RA does

They pull back your rug, check under the desk, look at the path between your bed and the door. Any dark spots, spills, or "crunchy" patches = cleaning fee. Dorm carpet is low-quality commercial grade that stains fast.

How to fix it (by stain type)

Fresh stain (last 48 hours): Blot with paper towels, then spray 1:1 white vinegar and water. Let it sit 10 minutes, blot again, repeat. Do not scrub — scrubbing spreads the stain deeper into the fibers.

Dried or dark stain: Folex Carpet Spot Remover ($5 on Amazon). Spray, let sit 30 seconds, blot. Students swear by this stuff on Reddit — it works on coffee, soda, and mystery dorm fluids that you don't want to identify.

Grease or food oil: Sprinkle baking soda on the spot, let it sit overnight, vacuum, then use Dawn dish soap + warm water with a cloth.

If the stain is truly dead: A $30 Bissell SpotClean portable carpet cleaner will save you from a $150 fine. Worth buying with roommates and splitting.

Trap #3: The Refrigerator That Grew a Science Experiment

Typical fine: $35–75

What the RA does

This is the #1 most common move-out fine. The RA opens your mini-fridge, finds mold, old food, or — worst case — the fridge was unplugged but never dried, and now the interior is growing something biological. They will charge you, and they will judge you.

How to fix it

Defrost the freezer at least 48 hours before inspection. Unplug the fridge, prop the door open with a towel, put a pot of hot water inside to speed up defrosting. Do not chip ice with a knife — you will puncture the coolant line and that's a $300 replacement.

Wipe all interior surfaces with warm water and baking soda. For tough mold: a very diluted bleach wipe (1 tablespoon bleach per cup of water) — then rinse with clean water.

Leave the door propped open for 12+ hours after cleaning. A closed, damp fridge = mold within 24 hours. The RA will open it and find exactly what you were trying to hide.

Trap #4: Broken or Stuck Blinds

Typical fine: $30–60

What the RA does

They check every window. Dorm blinds are flimsy — one wrong tug and a slat snaps, or the whole thing comes off the bracket. Broken blinds are an automatic replacement charge because housing has to call maintenance.

How to fix it

Single broken slat: Most dorm offices keep extras and will give you a replacement for free or $2. Ask at the front desk before you buy anything.

Blinds came off the wall: 90% of the time they just snap back into the clip. Look at the top rail — there's a small plastic clip on each end. Push the rail up into it until you hear the click. This takes 10 seconds and saves you $50.

If you can't fix it: Tell the RA before inspection. Some schools charge less for self-reported damages ($10–15) vs. discovered damages ($30–60). Honesty has a discount.

Trap #5: "I Forgot Something" — Trash and Leftovers

Typical fine: $25–50

What the RA does

They check: the closet, under the bed, drawers, behind the door, the bathroom cabinet, the freezer. Any leftover item — an old hoodie, half-empty shampoo bottle, a single bag of trash — triggers a "property removal fee."

How to avoid it — the three-pass system

First pass (3 days before): Remove everything large. Trash bags, extra furniture, clothes you're not taking home. Take the first load to your car or the dumpster now — don't wait.

Second pass (1 day before): Open every drawer, every cabinet, every closet. Check under the bed with a flashlight. Look behind the door. Look inside the shower caddy. Check the freezer for that one ice pop from September.

Third pass (morning of inspection): Walk the room like you're a detective looking for evidence. Look up (ceiling, light fixtures), look down (under furniture, behind legs), look inside (fridge, drawers, cabinets). Leave for 5 minutes, then walk back in like you've never seen it before — you'll spot things you've walked past all week.

Don't Waste Time on What Won't Get Charged

Not every imperfection triggers a fine. Here's what RAs are trained to charge for vs. what counts as normal wear and tear:

📌 What Actually Costs You

  • Will get charged: Large holes in walls, carpet stains, leftover trash/food, broken blinds, mold in fridge, missing keys or furniture
  • Won't get charged: Small nail holes from posters, faded or slightly dirty carpet, a few dust bunnies, slightly scuffed baseboards, normal paint fading

Know the difference. Don't spend three hours scrubbing baseboards that were already scuffed when you moved in. Do spend 20 minutes fixing the five things above. Your deposit is the prize for working smarter, not harder.

The One Trick That Saves You Every Time

Take a timestamped video walkthrough of your empty, cleaned room right before you leave. Show every wall, the open fridge, the carpet, the windows, the closet, the bathroom. Upload it to a private YouTube link or email it to yourself with the date in the subject line.

When housing sends you a $75 "excessive cleaning" charge three weeks later, you reply with the video link and one sentence: "The room was left in clean condition per the attached timestamped video. Please remove this charge."

This single habit has saved students literally thousands of dollars in false charges. Do not skip it.
