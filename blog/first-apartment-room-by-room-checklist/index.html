First Apartment Essentials: The Complete Room-by-Room Checklist for New Renters

✍️ By ChrisJune 29, 2026 · 9 min readFirst Apartment

You signed the lease. You paid the deposit. You have the keys. Now you're standing in an empty apartment with no furniture, no dishes, and a creeping sense that you forgot something important.

You're not alone. Every person moving into their first apartment faces the same problem: what do I actually need? Instagram makes it look like you need $8,000 worth of aesthetic furniture. Your parents' house has accumulated stuff over 20 years — they can't remember what they bought first.

According to a 2025 Apartment List survey, the average first-time renter spends between $1,500 and $3,500 furnishing their apartment. But the people who plan room-by-room — rather than impulse-buying — save an average of $600+ by avoiding duplicate purchases and "maybe I'll need this" mistakes.

This guide breaks down exactly what you need, organized by room. Not a generic shopping list — a real, prioritized system that tells you what to buy before move-in day, what can wait until week two, and what you should absolutely buy secondhand. Plus, there's a free interactive checklist at the end where you can track everything.

🛏️ Bedroom

Your bedroom is the first room you should set up. You'll be exhausted after moving day, and a functioning sleep space makes everything else bearable.

1. Mattress

If your apartment doesn't come with a bed, this is your first purchase. Don't skimp here — you spend a third of your life on it. A queen-size is the standard for apartments (60×80 inches). If you're on a tight budget, a quality queen mattress-in-a-box from brands like Zinus, Linenspa, or Nectar runs $200–400 and delivers to your door. Pro tip: Measure your bedroom door width and hallway turns before buying. Nothing worse than a mattress that doesn't fit up the stairs. 👉 Check mattress prices on Amazon

2. Bed Frame or Box Spring

A mattress on the floor traps moisture and will grow mold within months. A simple metal bed frame ($80–150) is cheap, sturdy, and gives you under-bed storage space. Platform beds don't need a box spring. If you want storage, look for a frame with built-in drawers or enough clearance for storage bins.

3. Mattress Protector + Sheet Set

A waterproof mattress protector ($15–25) is non-negotiable — spills, sweat, and accidents happen. For sheets, get deep pocket fitted sheets (8–12 inch) to fit over a mattress topper. One set to start is fine, but two means you always have clean sheets while the other is in the wash. Target Threshold and Amazon Basics are the most recommended budget picks.

4. Pillows & Comforter

Two pillows per person (one for sleeping, one for propping up). A mid-weight comforter or duvet works year-round for apartment living since you control the thermostat. Machine-washable is a must. Dark colors or patterns hide stains better than white.

5. Hangers (20-pack)

Velvet hangers are the best — they save space, clothes don't slip off, and they look uniform. A 20-pack runs about $12 on Amazon. Buy them before move-in day so you can unpack your clothes immediately.

6. Nightstand or Side Table

You need somewhere to put your phone, glasses, water, and alarm clock. A cheap IKEA LACK side table ($15) works perfectly. If you're handy, a secondhand nightstand from Facebook Marketplace ($10–30) does the same thing for less.

7. Lamp (Soft Lighting)

Apartment overhead lights are harsh. A bedside lamp with warm bulbs (2700–3000K) makes the room feel like home. Floor lamps work great for corners. Don't forget LED bulbs — they last years and use a fraction of the electricity.

8. Dresser or Storage Solution

Unless your closet is huge, you need drawer space. IKEA MALM or HEMNES dressers are the most popular apartment choices ($150–250). Secondhand is even better — Facebook Marketplace is full of them for $40–80. Just clean thoroughly before bringing inside.

🍳 Kitchen

You don't need a Pinterest-worthy kitchen. You need to be able to cook food, store leftovers, and wash dishes without making yourself miserable.

9. Cookware Set (3-Piece Starter)

A basic set with a non-stick frying pan (10-inch), saucepan (2-quart), and stockpot (6-quart) covers 90% of cooking needs. Skip the expensive 12-piece sets — you won't use half of them. Amazon Basics or IKEA 365+ are solid budget options. Never use metal utensils on non-stick pans — use silicone or wood. 👉 Check prices on Amazon

10. Chef's Knife (8-inch)

A single good knife is better than a block of 20 cheap ones. Spend $25–40 on a Victorinox Fibrox or Mercer Culinary — these are the knives culinary schools recommend. A cheap knife is actually more dangerous because you have to force it through food and it slips more easily.

11. Cutting Board

Get at least one large cutting board (12×16 inches minimum). Wood is gentler on knives but requires hand-washing. Plastic is dishwasher-safe. Many people keep two — one for raw meat, one for produce.

12. Plates, Bowls, Mugs (4-Pack Each)

You don't need fine china. A 4-pack of dinner plates, a 4-pack of bowls, and a 4-pack of mugs cover you for yourself plus guests. Corelleware is virtually unbreakable and lightweight — perfect for apartments. Total cost: about $50–60 for all three sets.

13. Silverware Set (20-Piece)

Forks, knives, and spoons for four people. A basic 20-piece set runs $15–25. Get ones with a nice weight — cheap flimsy silverware bends and feels terrible to use.

14. Basic Cooking Utensils

You need: spatula (silicone), tongs, wooden spoon, ladle, and a can opener. That's it. Don't buy a $40 "kitchen tool set" — pick these individually for under $30 total. Pro tip: buy the can opener before you need it. Nothing worse than being hungry with a sealed can and no way to open it.

15. Kitchen Knife Set (Bread Knife + Paring Knife)

Add a bread knife ($10–15) for slicing bread, tomatoes, and bagels without crushing them. A small paring knife ($5–10) is useful for fruit, peeling, and detail work. Together with your chef's knife, these three knives cover every kitchen task.

16. Colander

You'll use this more than you expect — draining pasta, rinsing beans, washing produce. A simple stainless steel or plastic colander costs $8–12. Get one that fits in your sink.

17. Dish Drying Rack + Dish Soap + Sponges

Unless you have a dishwasher, you need a drying rack. A two-tier rack holds plates on top and cups on the bottom. Don't forget the sponge and dish soap — you literally cannot wash dishes without them. Get a dish brush with a soap reservoir — it makes washing faster and keeps your hands out of greasy water.

18. Kitchen Trash Can

A simple step-can ($15–25) for the kitchen. Don't cheap out on trash bags — cheap bags rip, leak, and make a mess. Get 13-gallon bags that actually fit the can.

19. Microwave

Essential for reheating leftovers, popcorn, and quick meals. A basic 700–900 watt microwave costs $50–80. Get one with a handle, not a push-button release — push buttons break. 👉 Compare microwave prices on Amazon

20. Food Storage Containers

For leftovers and meal prep. Glass containers are better than plastic — they don't stain, don't warp in the microwave, and last years. A 10-piece glass set costs $15–25. Pyrex and Snapware are the most recommended brands.

Quick Kitchen Checklist

Also grab before move-in: coffee maker or kettle, reusable water bottle, cooking oil, salt & pepper, and dish towels. These small things are easy to forget but you'll want them on day one.
💡 Stock your pantry with basics before move-in: rice, pasta, canned tomatoes, olive oil, salt, pepper, and your favorite coffee or tea. This way you can cook a meal on day one without running to the store.

🛋️ Living Room

The living room is where you'll spend most of your awake time. But it's also the most expensive room to furnish — so prioritize smartly.

21. Sofa or Futon

Your biggest purchase after the mattress. A good sofa runs $300–800 new — but you can find perfectly good ones for $50–200 on Facebook Marketplace. Check for bed bugs before buying secondhand (look for dark spots in seams). If buying new, IKEA Kivik and Friheten are the most popular apartment sofas for a reason — they're comfortable, durable, and the covers are washable. Measure your doorways! A sofa that doesn't fit through the door is a classic first-apartment disaster. 👉 Shop sofas on Amazon

22. Coffee Table

This holds your coffee, remote, laptop, and feet. Secondhand is the smart move here — coffee tables are everywhere on Facebook Marketplace for $20–60. If buying new, IKEA LACK ($50) is the classic budget choice. Storage coffee tables (with drawers or shelves underneath) are worth the extra money.

23. Floor Lamp

Overhead lights in apartments are always too harsh. A floor lamp with three-way brightness completely changes the feel of a room. IKEA has good options for $15–30. LED bulbs with warm color temperature (2700K) make the space feel cozy.

24. Curtains or Blinds

Most apartments come with basic white blinds that offer zero privacy at night. Measure your windows before buying. Curtains that are too short or too narrow look terrible. Standard apartment windows need 84-inch curtains. Blackout curtains in the bedroom, light-filtering in the living room.

25. TV Stand or Media Console

If you own a TV, it needs to sit somewhere. A $30–80 stand from IKEA or Amazon works fine. Measure your TV width first — the stand should be wider than the TV for stability. Look for stands with cable management holes to hide the mess of wires.

26. Rug

Rugs define the space and make an apartment feel like home. A 5×7 or 6×9 rug is standard for a living room seating area. This is another item worth buying secondhand — people sell barely-used rugs for 20% of retail when they move. Cleaning tip: a rug pad underneath prevents slipping and adds cushion.

27. Power Strip with Surge Protection

Apartments never have outlets where you need them. Get a 6-outlet power strip with surge protection and USB ports ($15–25). You'll plug in your TV, lamp, phone charger, laptop, and possibly a gaming console. Get one with a 6-foot cord minimum.

🚿 Bathroom

The bathroom is small, but forgetting items here means awkward trips to the store in your pajamas.

28. Shower Curtain + Liner + Hooks

Your apartment will have a shower rod but almost certainly no curtain. You need both a fabric curtain (outer layer) and a plastic/PEVA liner (inner layer) that goes inside the tub. The liner keeps water off the bathroom floor. A set with hooks included runs $15–30. Get a fabric liner if possible — plastic ones stick to your skin while showering. 👉 Check prices on Amazon

29. Bath Mat (Non-Slip)

Stepping out of a shower onto a cold, wet floor is miserable. A bath mat with a non-slip rubber backing gives you a dry place to stand. Wash it every 2–3 weeks or it gets musty. Two mats means one can be in the wash.

30. Towel Set (2 Bath + 2 Hand + 2 Washcloth)

A set of 6 towels (2 large, 2 medium, 2 small) covers you between laundry days. 100% cotton (Turkish or Egyptian) is the gold standard. Skip microfiber — it doesn't dry as well as cotton for body drying.

31. Toilet Paper (Stock Up)

Buy a 12-pack or 24-pack before you need it. Running out of toilet paper at 11 PM is a rite of passage you can skip. This is the one item where buying in bulk is actually smart for apartment living.

32. Plunger + Toilet Brush

Buy these before you need them. Not after. A clogged toilet at 2 AM with no plunger is a unique form of misery. A plunger and a toilet brush set costs under $15 for both. Put them next to the toilet — out of sight means out of mind when you really need one.

33. Bathroom Trash Can (Small + Lid)

A small lidded trash can ($8–15) in the bathroom is essential. Get one with a lid — it hides the contents and contains odors. The simple step-can style is perfect.

34. Medicine Cabinet or Storage Shelves

If your apartment bathroom doesn't have a medicine cabinet, you need storage for toiletries. An over-the-toilet shelf or a simple standing caddy works. Command hooks on the wall for towels and robes help too.

35. Full-Length Mirror

Most apartment bathrooms only have a small medicine cabinet mirror. A full-length mirror ($15–30) is essential for checking your outfit before leaving. Lean it against the wall or mount it on a door using Command strips.

🧹 Cleaning & Supplies

Nobody gets excited about cleaning supplies. But having them on move-in day means you can clean before you unpack — and your apartment will feel like yours.

36. Vacuum Cleaner

If your apartment has carpet, a vacuum is non-negotiable. A stick vacuum (cordless) is perfect for apartments — lightweight, easy to store, and good enough for small spaces. The Amazon Basics stick vacuum or a Bissell Featherweight runs $30–50. If you have all hard floors, a Swiffer WetJet + a broom is cheaper. 👉 Compare vacuums on Amazon

37. Broom + Dustpan

Even if you have a vacuum, a broom is faster for quick cleanups. A cheap $10 set works fine. Get a dustpan with a rubber lip — it sits flush against the floor and catches everything.

38. All-Purpose Cleaner + Microfiber Cloths

One spray cleaner (like Mrs. Meyer's or Method) handles counters, tables, sinks, and windows. Microfiber cloths ($8–12 for a 12-pack) are reusable, streak-free, and machine washable. They're better than paper towels for cleaning and cheaper in the long run.

39. Trash Bags (Bulk)

Buy a box of 13-gallon kitchen bags, a box of 4–8 gallon bags for bathroom/desk trash cans, and you're set for months. Glad ForceFlex or Kirkland (Costco) are the most tear-resistant brands. Cheap bags will rip when you're taking out the trash and you'll regret saving $2.

40. Laundry Hamper + Detergent

You need something to carry your clothes to the laundry room. A mesh duffle-style hamper is better than a hard plastic bin — it's easier to carry and folds flat when empty. For detergent, pods or sheets are easier than liquid for apartment laundry. Tide or Persil pods — skip the generic brands.

🧰 Tools & Safety

These are the items you won't think about until you need them — and when you need them, you'll be very glad you have them.

41. Basic Tool Kit

You will assemble IKEA furniture. You need a hammer, screwdriver set (Phillips and flathead), tape measure, and adjustable wrench. A small 12-piece tool kit costs $15–25 on Amazon and covers everything an apartment renter needs. Don't buy tools individually — the kit is cheaper than the sum of its parts. 👉 Check tool kit prices on Amazon

42. Flashlight + Batteries

Power outages happen in every apartment eventually. A simple LED flashlight ($8–12) with extra AAA batteries kept in a kitchen drawer saves you from navigating your apartment by phone light. A hand-crank or solar flashlight is even better — no batteries to die.

43. First Aid Kit

A small kit with Band-Aids, antiseptic wipes, gauze, medical tape, tweezers, ibuprofen, and allergy medication. A pre-assembled kit costs $10–15. Keep it in the kitchen or a visible spot — not buried in a closet.

44. Fire Extinguisher

One of the most overlooked apartment items. A small kitchen fire extinguisher costs $15–25. Keep it under the kitchen sink, not in a pantry or closet. If your stove catches fire, you have seconds to react — not minutes to find the extinguisher. Check the pressure gauge monthly.

45. Command Hooks (Assorted Pack)

These are the most versatile item in your apartment. Use them for: keys by the door, coats, towels, string lights, picture frames, cables, oven mitts, and bags. An assorted pack with 10–15 hooks of various sizes costs $8–12 and you'll use every single one. Follow the instructions: clean the wall with alcohol, press firmly for 30 seconds, wait an hour before hanging. Following this means they won't fall off at 3 AM.

46. Scissors

Sounds obvious but you'd be surprised. Opening packages, cutting tape, trimming tags — you will use scissors every day. Keep a pair in the kitchen and one in your desk drawer. A $5 pair from Target is perfectly fine.

🔌 Utilities & Internet

These aren't physical items you buy at the store, but they're just as essential. Set them up before move-in day.

47. Internet Setup

Schedule your internet installation at least 2 weeks before move-in. Providers like Xfinity, Spectrum, and AT&T often have appointment windows of 3–7 days. Without internet, you can't work, stream, or set up your smart devices. Buy your own WiFi router instead of renting from the ISP ($10–15/month rental vs $60–80 one-time purchase). You'll save $120–180 per year. 👉 Compare routers on Amazon
💡 Use our Rent Affordability Calculator to budget for utilities before signing your lease — internet, electricity, water, and renter's insurance add $150–300/month to your rent.

48. Utility Setup (Electric, Gas, Water)

Some apartments include utilities in the rent; most don't. Call the local utility companies at least a week before move-in to set up your accounts. You'll typically need: your ID, social security number, lease agreement, and a deposit (usually equal to one month's bill). Ask about budget billing — it averages your payments so you don't get a $400 AC bill in August.

49. Renter's Insurance

This is not optional. Many landlords require it, and even if they don't, you're an idiot for skipping it. Renter's insurance costs $10–20/month and covers your belongings if there's a fire, burglary, or water damage. Lemonade, State Farm, and Progressive offer online quotes in under 5 minutes. Your landlord's insurance only covers the building — not your stuff inside it.

50. Change of Address + Mail Forwarding

Set up USPS mail forwarding at least a week before moving. It's free and takes 5 minutes at usps.com/move. Also update your address with your bank, credit card companies, employer, school, and any subscription services. Pro tip: Take a photo of your new mailbox key or code and text it to yourself. You'll forget the code by day two.

📦 Moving Day Essentials

These items aren't for your apartment — they're for surviving the chaos of moving day itself.

51. Moving Boxes (Not Garbage Bags)

Do not use garbage bags for moving. They rip, they're hard to label, and things get crushed. Get proper cardboard moving boxes from Home Depot or Lowe's ($1.50–3 each). Or better yet, check your local liquor store — they give away sturdy boxes for free. Small boxes for heavy items (books, tools), large boxes for light items (linens, pillows).

52. Packing Tape + Marker

You can't pack boxes without tape. Get a tape gun with dispenser ($5) — it's worth the money. Sharpies in two colors: black for contents, red for "FRAGILE — OPEN FIRST." Label boxes on the top and one side so you can read the contents when they're stacked.

53. "Open First" Box

Pack one box with everything you'll need in the first 24 hours: toilet paper, paper plates, one set of utensils, a towel, toothbrush + toothpaste, phone charger, a change of clothes, scissors, and a trash bag. Keep this box in the passenger seat — not in the moving truck. When you're exhausted at midnight and the moving truck is partially unloaded, this box saves you from digging through 20 boxes to find your toothbrush.

54. Cleaning Supplies (For Move-In Day Cleaning)

Before you unload furniture, clean the apartment. Previous tenants left dust, grime, and mystery stains. Bring an all-purpose cleaner, paper towels, sponge, and trash bags. Wipe down cabinets, shelves, windowsills, and the inside of the fridge before putting anything away. It's 10x harder to clean behind furniture after it's placed.

55. Snacks + Water + Music Speaker

Moving is physically exhausting. You will get hungry, dehydrated, and bored. Pack a bag with protein bars, water bottles, and a Bluetooth speaker. Music makes the work go faster. Order pizza for your friends who help you move. They'll remember the pizza more than the lifting.

✦ ✦ ✦

One more thing: The single biggest mistake new renters make isn't forgetting a specific item — it's buying everything at full price before they've moved in. About 40% of "essential" apartment items can be bought secondhand for 70% less. Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, and Buy Nothing groups are full of people selling barely-used furniture because they're moving out. The only things you should always buy new: mattress, pillows, sheets, towels, toilet brush, and kitchen sponges. Everything else — sofa, coffee table, dresser, lamps, rugs, kitchen gadgets — can wait for the secondhand score.
